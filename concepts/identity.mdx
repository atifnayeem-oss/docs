The **Caller** is who is paying: a person and their saved cards, recognized by the phone number
they are calling from. That number arrives at Ringup on a trusted header the platform templates in
at call time, so it never passes through the language model. Which business they reached is a
separate identity, see [Merchant Identity](/concepts/merchant-identity).

## The Caller record

The Caller is Ringup's record of a person and their saved cards, keyed by phone number and shared
across every merchant (a wallet, not one merchant's contact list). The number rides the
`X-Caller-Number` header, templated from the platform's caller-ID variable. Ringup looks the Caller
up:

```json
{
  "known": true,
  "name": "Alex",
  "card": { "brand": "VISA", "last_4": "5858", "exp_month": 7, "exp_year": 2028 }
}
```

A recognized Caller is offered their saved Card by name and last four, with no identity questions.
An unknown caller is offered a texted pay link instead; once they pay, they become a Caller and are
recognized on every call after that, at any Ringup merchant. Recognition is automatic: you never
manage a Caller object, it just happens.

## Recognize at the start of the call

`identify` needs only the caller's phone number, and that arrives the instant the call connects,
before the caller has said a word. So call it at the **start** of the call, not just when payment
begins. For a recognized caller it hands you their name and saved card up front, which you use to
personalize the whole call: greet them by name, and skip re-asking for anything you already know
(for example, never ask "what name should I put this under?" when `identify` already returned it).

Calling it early also moves the lookup off the payment moment, where a pause in the conversation is
more noticeable than during the greeting.

Some integrations wire recognition for you, so it happens without any tool call in your agent. On
the others you invoke `identify` yourself, and invoking it at the greeting is what unlocks the
personalized, no-repeat-questions experience.

## Why the caller comes from the call, not the model

A model-generated `phone_number` argument can be mistyped, hallucinated, or prompt-injected by a
hostile caller. A header set by the platform's template engine cannot. The tools accept no caller
argument at all; there is nothing to spoof.

<Warning>
Dashboard and web test calls carry no real caller ID, so the platform's caller-ID template resolves
to nothing. For those, pin a test caller explicitly. Real inbound phone calls resolve the caller
natively.
</Warning>
